Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi (1869-1948): A Biography

Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi, commonly known as Mahatma Gandhi or Bapu, was born on October 2, 1869, in Porbandar, Gujarat, India. Raised in a devout Hindu family, Gandhi's principles of truth, nonviolence, and self-discipline were instilled in him from an early age.

Gandhi studied law in London and later practiced in South Africa, where he became an advocate for civil rights, fighting against racial discrimination. It was during this time that he developed the concept of nonviolent resistance, which he later termed "Satyagraha."

Returning to India in 1915, Gandhi emerged as a prominent leader in the Indian National Congress, advocating for India's independence from British rule. His philosophy of nonviolence and civil disobedience became the guiding force behind the Indian independence movement.

Tragically, on January 30, 1948, Mahatma Gandhi was assassinated by Nathuram Godse, a Hindu nationalist who opposed Gandhi's tolerant views. Despite his physical absence, Gandhi's legacy endures, inspiring generations with his teachings of truth, love, and nonviolence.

Gandhi's birthday, October 2nd, is celebrated as Gandhi Jayanti, and his philosophy continues to be a guiding light for those who seek peaceful solutions to conflicts and social injustices.

Philosophy

Nonviolent Resistance (Satyagraha)

Gandhi's core philosophy was based on the principle of nonviolent resistance, or "Satyagraha." He believed in the power of truth and love to overcome injustice.

Sarvodaya

Gandhi advocated for the welfare of all, promoting the idea of "Sarvodaya" where the upliftment of the weakest in society is the true measure of progress.

Swadeshi and Self-Sufficiency

Gandhi emphasized economic self-sufficiency and the use of locally-made goods through the "Swadeshi" movement, aiming to reduce dependence on foreign imports.

Key Events

1888

Legal Practice in London

Gandhi travels to London to study law, qualifying as a barrister in 1888.

1893

South Africa Activism

Gandhi begins his civil rights activism in South Africa, fighting against racial discrimination and injustice.

1915

Return to India

Gandhi returns to India and becomes a leader in the Indian National Congress, advocating for India's independence.

1919

Jallianwala Bagh Massacre

Gandhi launches nonviolent protests against the brutal massacre in Amritsar, condemning British rule.

1930

Salt March

Gandhi leads a 240-mile march to the Arabian Sea in protest of British salt taxes, a symbol of civil disobedience.

1942

Quit India Movement

Gandhi launches the Quit India Movement, demanding an end to British rule in India.

1947

Indian Independence

India gains independence, and Gandhi works towards communal harmony during the partition.

1948

Mahatma Gandhi is assassinated by Nathuram Godse, a Hindu nationalist who opposed his tolerant views.
